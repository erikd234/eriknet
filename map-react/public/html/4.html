I woke up by the hot spring and I went for a snorkel, Adam and Kai picked up snorkel in tin Athens for 10 euros. There were so many fish since they all flock to the heated thermal vents. If I had a speargun I think I could eat fish for a month without much difficulty.

I sat in the hot springs for a while before we started to pack up the tents.

A few French people came down to the hot springs while we were leaving. I had my first conversation with someone from France in person. It was awesome, understanding and being understood, in French. They ended up inviting us to stay in their garden if we go to Bordeaux, France. They were doing a bike tour in Greece with e-bikes and a van. They would drive around Europe with a van and have their e bikes in the back. They said it was the funnest way to travel.

The night before, I got the WhatsApp of the Alex, the Greek professional Chess player. He had invited us to his villa in the mountains the night before so we would not have to camp at the hot-springs but we refused since we were already set up and it would have been difficult to pack up and leave. But the next day we decided to take him up on the offer and go for some Greek food. We took our time leaving the hot springs.

We sat in the Villa out looking at the Ocean and mountains from the view from his villa patio for the rest of the evening. We just sat there relaxing with the view for hours. All of us dumbfounded for how we ended up here.

Feasting like Greek gods

Time just melts away. Even though we are doing literally nothing, sitting on the patio with the view makes you somehow feel busy. Like nothing else matters but standing out at the ocean. I think I could just sit on the porch for the next two months and be really happy. Life in this little town is really really quiet. All you hear is cats fighting in the street and the buzzing of the massive bees.

A bird eye view of the villa

While sitting out this porch these are some interesting things I learned about Greece and Greek culture.

  1. They all make their own in-village olive oil. And they have way way too much of it. More than they know what to do with. The use olive oil as fire starter since they have too much. They fill giant coke bottles of olive oil. Alex said he had about 10 litres of olive oil in the villa.

  2. High-schools all across Greece have a yearly tradition where the students lock the school during weeks, they lock the windows, and basically bars are themselves in the school. When Alex was in high school, he was the one that organize them in his village. He said he once locked the school for 2 months. The students create demands for the teachers for improvements in the school. One year, one of his demands was to change the shape of the cheese cake in the cafeteria from square to circle. Another was fixing a broken section of roof. This tradition blew my mind and puts North American grad pranks is to shame. The students all band together to close the school for 1 - 6 weeks, created a list of demands, and the students even sleep in the school to make sure the teachers can’t break back into the school. Usually one of two things happen. First, the teacher agree to the demands and fix the issues that the students are protesting to get solved. Second, the teachers get pissed off and call the police to come open the school. Sometimes both happen. Alex explained to me that students in Greece are very political which came after a massacre in a university during the Greece dictator ship in the 60s and 70s. I don’t know much more than then but if you are interested check it out and let me know the full story.

  3. Greek food tastes great. I have always hated eating olives, Greek salad, but now I cant get enough of it. its amazing to go from village to village and they give you the olive oil they make in their backyard, or use the olives made a few kms away. It makes enjoy olives a lot more.

  4. Careful when you drink the water bottles. They fill the water bottles with Tsipuro (Greek hard liquor 60% alcohol, made from island grapes), and we almost made our morning coffee with it since it was in a normal bottle. I give each bottle a sniff before I drink from it 😂

  5. Greek coffee! To make Greek style coffee you just throw coffee grounds in a little pot filled with water and boil it. It’s very easy to make and I will now be making it each day of this trip. After you drink the coffee inside the cup there’s a bunch of leftover coffee grounds, since we never used a filter for the coffee. Apparently what many Greek people do is the flip the cup upside after the coffee is drunk and the grounds is all that remains. You wait 5 or so minutes, and the coffee grounds forms into drawings on the rim of the small Greek coffee cups and these drawings predict your future. Mine said I would meet a girl in a volcano. I’ll let you know if that happens haha.

I am starting to learn Greek now too, I have been inspired, the language is beautiful and the characters as well. Let’s see how far I can get in the next couple weeks. Communicating with locals is really hard without Greek, especially away from the city.

Best Greek salad I’ve ever had

After passing hours sitting and staring at the view, and all you can eat Greek food, prepared for us by Alex, we decided to hike/ bike up the volcano mountain that we were staying on. We tried to catch the sunset from the top of the mountain.

We prepared our bikes and started to bike up the same really steep mountain as yesterday (a different road than last time however). And biked up about 400 meters in elevation of 20% gradient switchbacks to the trail head. We then tried to hike to the top of a volcano.

view from near the top of the switchbacks

The trail was wonderful, we got to enjoy the mountain valleys and see the city glow of Athens from a far. We missed the sunset since the hike was longer than we thought and begun hiking in the dark. I got to see the type of trails Kai had braved on his bike the day before, while Adam and I stayed to the road, and was really happy we stayed on the road in the previous mountain trek.

The sun fell and we hiked back down to the trailhead in the dark. Thank god for offline GPS and maps! It was straightforward to make our way back. We then descended the switchbacks of doom by bike in the dark. That was so much fun. The narrow roads, the lights from the four of us biking down, and the lights from the little villages on the neighbouring island. Chefs kiss.

Volcano trail after dark

So why Island of Lotus eaters?

Here is the rundown from Wikipedia

“ In Greek mythology, the lotus-eaters (Greek: λωτοφάγοι, translit. lōtophágoi) were a race of people living on an island dominated by the lotus tree, a plant whose botanical identity is uncertain. The lotus fruits and flowers were the primary food of the island and were a narcotic, causing the inhabitants to sleep in peaceful apathy. After they ate the lotus, they would forget their home and loved ones, and only long to stay with their fellow lotus-eaters. Those who ate the plant never cared to report, nor return”

From what we have seen. That’s Methena. We might be here a little while :)
