#37 - First day in Italia.

June 25th

Yesterday I said, I would answer the question. Why am I now travelling alone?

If you haven’t travelled alone before, It may sound scary. About exactly one year ago - I did my first solo backpacking trip in the south of Mexico.

My favourite place in the mexico trip. A mountain village in Oaxaca

Before this trip, travelling alone sounded terrifying to me as well . That’s partly why did it. I wanted to do something to get out of my comfort zone.

That trip was fantastic - I absolutely love the freedom of being alone and making all the decisions. If I wasn’t having a good time, it was my fault. And I had the power to change anything I was doing in the trip. It was also a way for me to develop more independence and immerse myself in another culture. For two weeks I pretty much only spoke Spanish with Mexicans (and one Belgian).

Now for me, bike touring solo was a whole other level. I was so sure about bike touring in Europe before this trip, that I convince myself if Adam bailed on me pre-trip, I would just do the whole thing solo. But deep down, I was afraid because I had no idea how to bike tour. I’m glad Adam did not bail on the idea (tho he almost did haha (❤️Adam)).

As I mentioned before, I didn’t even know how to camp. Now, as I write this, I’m currently the only person on a beach in Italy, at 11pm. I only found this place to sleep at 6 PM. I never know where I’m going to sleep until about 6 PM, 90% of the days.

All alone on the beach

Adam and I get along great while travelling, but after a month of travelling around Greece, we both felt like we wanted this control again. I would want to visit one place, or I would want to go - he wants to stop, or opposites. Basically we each were feeling we wanted to have more control.

Now the prospect of going alone doesn’t scare me (still a little bit but a lot less than before) because I figured out how bike touring works (thanks kai). I have a lot more confidence in myself. I know I will be fine all alone, even in the worst situation’s.

So that brings me to the other big reason - I want to pose myself a little challenge. How will I be a full month or two fully alone? I’ve been biking at least 6 or 7 hours a day. And it will just be me with me on the bike (maybe with a song in my headphones or random people like today when I biked with a 74 year old Italian man for an hour)

And finally, since I picked Italy as my solo adventure - I pretty get to talk Italian nonstop for one month. Molto molto bene.

For the last two days I’ve only spoken in Italian - I’m even talking to myself in Italian. In fact, the most English I have done is writing this post by far.

I want to see how fluent I can become an Italian after one month of full immersion. I’m already able to have amazing conversations with the locals here.

They all are so surprised to see a Canadian speaking Italian. They always ask why do I speak Italian and I never know what to say. “Cause I like it” I often reply. I don’t have a reason to be able to speak Italian other than I thought it was a cool idea to learn the language for this trip. Maybe i will just say that from now on…

So that’s why I’m travelling solo now :)

On the 24th…

I woke up in the morning on the ferry. It was an amazing sleep. I was the only one except for a family in a tent to sleep the entire night on top of the ferry. I used on of the lay down deck chairs. In the sky I saw the stars, and for the first night I was able to use my sleeping bag - the sea breeze kept me cool. Normally it’s too hot.

waking up one the ferry

Next… my first impressions off the boat were that this part of Italy is very flat. I didn’t see a single mountain. Also, the port had many cargo ships which I didn’t like that much. I think this is the name of the game with port cities.

I got off the ferry and ventured into the town of Brindisi.

the port

My first interaction with someone Italian was a shop owner. I asked him where I can get some breakfast since it was 8 AM. I found it funny that he told me. “Oh yes, there’s a magnificent bar to eat at down the road. They have the best breakfast.” He was gesticulating with his hands a lot which I love - them using their hands a lot is 100% true and its amazing to watch. I now am using my hands a lot too.

I was confused because in Italy “bars”, are places that give you coffee and breakfast and sweets kind of like a coffee shop or a bakery in Canada.

Another thing was I came and arrived on Sunday- and almost every store was closed. I guess it was early in the morning, but it sort of felt like a ghost town. This made it even harder to find food. In Greece they close on Sunday too but normally it’s in the really small villages. Brindisi is a big port town. I was very surprised to see many things closed.

I continued to the bar, where I asked for recommendations, and had my first meal in Italy. Cornetto and cappuccino. Both were amazing and better than what I’ve had in Canada.

first food in Italy

But Brindisi was stunning. The people who told me Corfu looks like Italy were correct. They have the same style of buildings.

street of Brindisi

Already I’m noticing so many things about Italians, every stereotype is true that I’ve heard. They love to use their hands, they get so emotional and the stores and yell, they all dress well, and they’re all super super kind. When I talk to them Italian, they’re super helpful. If I don’t know where something is, they’ll do everything in their power to help me find what I’m looking for.

I left Brindisi and headed south to a city called Lecce. An Italian man I met in Mani, Greece - told me I had to go there. Since it was so close to Brindisi. I thought I would go check it out.

flat roads the whole way.

The ride to Lecce was completely flat - just old farm roads. Not very exciting riding - but easy riding. When I’m on the flats, I missed the hills. When I’m on the hills I beg for the flats. Funny how that works.

At one point on my way, I saw a bunch of smoke on the road ahead. One of the fields in front of me was on fire. Obviously, I didn’t go down this road. I backtracked and went to go talk to somebody who live in a nearby house. I was telling an elderly woman that there’s a fire there and ask her if we should call someone and if it’s an emergency.

I was so surprised by her calm, nonchalant reaction. She said it’s nothing. It was not an emergency. It’s only bad if the fire reaches the wheat fields. And finally that we don’t need to do anything and there’s nothing we can do except just let the fire burn. She said it gets really dry and this is super common. This very much surprised me. I continue it on a different road that was not smoking.

Eventually, I got to Lecce. It’s definitely an extremely beautiful city.

First thing I see in Lecce (Also where I think I left my water bottle ☹️ )

When I got there, I was a little confused on what the hell I should do lol. I am aimlessly biked around the town. I didn’t really want to pay to enter the big monuments. And I definitely didn’t wanna pay for hostel. At this point, I prefer my tent.

My slogan is… Pay for food, not for accommodations.

Lecce

More of Lecce

So I spent the afternoon and Lecce biking around. Then I left again for a different beach.

It’s always nice to camp on the beaches

I went to Porto Cesareo.

Again, the writing was easy flat, and not very exciting.

I arrived in Porto Cesareo stopped and filled my water at a pizzeria. Then some locals who worked at the pizzeria recommended a beach I should go to to sleep with a tent and not be bothered by anyone.

Porto Cesareo

I realized here that I left one of my water bottles in Lecce… Darn.

I went to the beach, it was very full of people, but very nice.

I cook dinner, talked to Italians who were all so curious about the bike, and watch the sunset.

cooking pasta + sunset

Once the beach emptied, I found a hidden spot for my tent.

It was just me, and two Italian teenagers were fishing. We chatted for a while while they fished. They taught me some Italian words in their local dialect of Italian called Salentino.

I learned that between friends, they all speak this Italian dialect which is not comprehensible to people who speak Italian. And proper public places in with strangers - they will speak Italian.

It was fun - they gave me some recommendations for food to eat and now my list is growing.
