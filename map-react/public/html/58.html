#58 - Sleeping beside a bomb.

July 24th

June 24th

I woke up in the morning and I wasn’t too cold in the night. The temperature went down to about 7°C. And with all my clothes on and sleeping bag, I was warm all night.

I’m curious to see what’s the coldest temperature I can withstand before gets really uncomfortable.

It’s getting colder up here in the alps in the next couple days. It’s gonna reach down to 3° even at lower elevation.

Let’s see how I do with my tent .

After a lot of climbing out of the day before I just took it easy. I rode on the top of the mountain all morning. It was raining on and off.

For a while, I sat in the mountains .

I had a beautiful Campspot last night, but if I just biked another kilometre I would’ve had an incredible view of the mountains even better than the one I had.

When I was wet and it was windy, it got pretty cold. There was no sun and at this elevation 2000 m. It was about 10°C at the warmest part of the day.

I feel just like at home in Vancouver . Wet, rainy, and cold biking.

The ride is actually so beautiful. The mountains in insanely impressive to see.

This was probably one of the coolest routes to bike down just because I was high up it is. The great part is there so many more opportunities for rides like this. Rides even better than this.

I took a few pretty photos.

I felt as if I was in a very remote area and then I stumbled across this little house. They sold cheese. They had this tiny little church on the edge of the cliff. It made for epic photo.

I think this is probably one of the best photos of ever taken at least one of my favourite photos.

When I passed by, I ask if they also sell coffee.

They said they don’t they just sell cheese. But, since it was raining and freezing cold, they invited me in for coffee anyway.

I accepted the offer. I went into their small mountain home and we talked about my trip and where I should go in the alps and I got to see what it’s like in a little mountain home in northern Italy.

Once it stopped raining, I left their house. And of course, another 20 minutes in fog and rain came again to block my view.

I quickly got to the descent, which I did in the rain. I arrived at little village called Monno. Once again to avoid the rain I went to a bar. And I sat in this bar for maybe five hours I kept ordering food and coffee and chatting with the staff. There was no one else there.

Finally, I left the bar and continued down the descent and recorded a beautiful GoPro video of the decent. I will one day upload if I have Internet.

Then, and I arrived at my destination Ponte di lengo.

A small village in between two mountain passes. the Tonale pass and the Gavia pass.

Both are huge mountain climbs. One to 2600 metres high, the other 2200.

The next day I would go one of these mountain passes.

When I got to Ponte di legno it was raining hard, and there was thunder and lightning.

I went to a pizzeria for three or four hours until 10 o’clock I ate two pizzas. The sun had set. It was still raining., and I had no idea where I was going to sleep.

These are always the moments that are most fun for me. Everything seems to happen when it’s late at night dark and you still don’t know where you’re going to sleep.

The guy at the pizza shop asked me where I’m going to sleep. I told him I don’t know yet. Then I asked if he knew a place that maybe was protected from the heavy rain. He recommended I go to the bus station.

I thought that’s probably not gonna work…

But I told him thank you and after I left the pizzeria, I decided to give it a try anyway.

I biked in the dark down the road to the bus station.

I couldn’t find it. I’d like to pass it three times.

Finally I arrived, and there wasn’t really much. Just a coffee shop/bar.

I walked in and asked the guy at the bar, if you knew a place where I could sleep that was covered because it was raining and there was thunder, and about 7° or 8° outside.

He really had no idea. It was clear that I couldn’t sleep in the station. It wasn’t set up like this.

Then incomes this guy… now how do I describe him?

He was missing a tooth, he was missing a finger, and he seemed a little crazy at first. Maybe he had a bit to drink already.

I’m pretty open minded guy, but his appearance initially startled me a bit. And also the reaction of the barman, and how the barren behaved regarding him.

But the man said I could sleep in his home on the couch. He was with his other friend.

This seems like a good option, but I need to find out more about this guy because his appearance actually made me think this would not be a good idea.

We talked about his family what he does all the stuff casually. I didn’t hear any red flags.

He wanted to stay at the bar and keep drinking - then he said he could show me where he lives and I could stay there while he goes back to the bar.

So we walk together and kept talking. After our conversations, I came conclusion that this was probably a low risk. He had other family members living in the house. The barman knew him his friend seem like a nice guy.

His house was very close to the bar.

Another good sign…

Turns out he lives in the basement. To get in the house you go around the back. So we carried my bike down the stairs.

Then, when we open the door and go to his house, I was once again worried.

The first thing I see, is loads and loads of weapons on the walls, knives, guns, grenades. I’m thinking what the hell am I doing? But of course I ask about them

. I’m ready to get out of here fast.

But it turns out all these weapons are actually first world war relics. Antique weapons from World War I.

This calms me a bit. Then he shows me the real collection. Holly smokes it’s like 100 bombs and grenades on the wall.

I was shocked to say the least.

Then he told me HE found these all himself.

His hobby is he takes a metal detector, hikes in the Alps for weeks and finds old war weapons and equipment. When he finds grenades and bombs he HIMSELF defuses them…. And he taught himself how to defuse these bombs.

He explained to me how he defuses them and I almost lost it. I was actually equally impressed as I was startled.

I decided to stay, which only a 2/10 chance I get murdered in the night. Figuring that murdering me didn’t make much sense with all the witnesses.

My then only real danger was one of these many hand grenades or bombshell blows up…. But he said he had been doing this for 16 years and he’s only lost one finger (unrelated to exploding bombs)

Anyway. I was careful not to knock anything over.

I had two options… sleep next to a bomb or sleep in 7 degrees and pouring rain…

Crazy.

I write it like this but the guy was actually very sweet as I got to know him. He was a little bit crazy, but aren’t we all.

So just so you know, I survive the night. We actually went for breakfast in the morning. He was super helpful. Help me find a store to buy some pants for when it gets cold again. And we chatted for a while about life.

That’s all for now,

-Erik
